Manifesto

We live surrounded by intelligence that makes us more efficient but leaves us more alone. The very technologies that promised to connect us have become walls between the moments that matter most.

Distance defeats love not through space, but through time — the accumulation of unnoticed changes, unspoken struggles, missed signals that caring eyes might have caught. We've built a world where being present requires being physically present, where understanding demands constant vigilance.

Arthur C. Clarke, the visionary science fiction author and futurist whose ideas — from geostationary satellites to 2001: A Space Odyssey — reshaped how we imagine the future.

In 1945, young Arthur C. Clarke envisioned satellites that would let television and communication reach "the entire planet". He didn't imagine efficiency — he imagined connection. A world where distance couldn't defeat human understanding. Yet the internet age gave us global reach but local isolation.

The recent breakthrough in ambient intelligence feels like standing at the edge of something that could fundamentally change how we exist together. Yet most builders have fallen back on what feels familiar: productivity, optimisation, the individual as the unit of measurement.

We've trained machines to understand human speech, but we've forgotten to teach them about human care.

Love is the invisible architecture that holds everything together. It's defeated by distance, by time, by the simple fact that caring deeply doesn't grant us the superhuman ability to be everywhere at once. Technology promised to solve this. Instead, it gave us productivity tools that make us more efficient at being alone.

Idiom One begins with a different question: What if AI didn't just make you better at being you, but better at being connected to the people who matter? What if intelligence could be distributed across relationships, not just concentrated in individuals?

It's about presence. The kind that turns the continuous stream of life into opportunities for understanding. Technology as trust. Intelligence as amplified compassion.

Because the people who are crazy enough to believe that technology should make us more human, not less, are the ones who will build the future we actually want to live in.

Fei Lin (Andrew) Guo
Melbourne, Sep 15, 2025
